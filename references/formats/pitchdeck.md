# Pitch Deck Format, Investor Decks That Get Funded

## Goal

A 10-15 slide deck that makes investors feel they would be stupid NOT to invest. Not a research presentation. Not a product demo. A story where the outcome feels inevitable.

## When to Use

- Seed / Series A fundraising
- Startup pitch competitions
- Internal innovation pitches (corporate "intrapreneurship")
- Any context where you need money and the audience sees 500+ decks per year

## The 12-Slide Template

### Slide 1: The One-Liner
Your company name + the single sentence that explains what you do.
```
Bad:  "GradZip: A Novel Framework for Lossless Gradient Compression"
Good: "GradZip: We make distributed training 3x cheaper with zero accuracy loss."
```
The investor must understand your business in 5 seconds.

### Slide 2: The Problem
Whose problem? How big? How painful?
- One sentence stating the problem from the USER's perspective.
- One statistic quantifying the pain.
- One quote from a real person experiencing the problem (if you have it).

### Slide 3: The Solution
What you built. Show, do not tell.
- One screenshot or mockup of the product.
- Three bullet points max: what it does, who it's for, why it's different.

### Slide 4: The Market
How big is the opportunity?
- TAM (Total Addressable Market): all possible customers.
- SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market): customers you can reach.
- SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): customers you will realistically capture.
- Bottom-up, not top-down. "X million developers × $Y per seat" not "the AI market is $Z billion."

### Slide 5: The Insight
Why now? Why you?
- What do you know that nobody else knows?
- What changed in the world that makes this possible now?
- This is the "Shannon filter" for startups. If the answer is "we work harder," you have no insight.

### Slide 6: The Product
What does the user actually see?
- 2-3 screenshots with captions.
- One feature = one slide. Do not cram 10 features onto one screen.
- If you have a demo, this is where you mention it. "Demo available on request" is weak. Embed a GIF or link.

### Slide 7: Traction
Proof that people want this.
- Revenue (if you have it), users, pilot customers, LOIs, waitlist signups.
- Growth curve (even if small, direction matters more than magnitude).
- Specific numbers, not "growing fast."

### Slide 8: Business Model
How do you make money?
- Pricing model (per seat, usage-based, marketplace take rate).
- One sentence on unit economics (CAC, LTV if you have them).
- Comparables: "X charges $Y, we charge $Z and deliver more value because..."

### Slide 9: Competition
Who else is doing this? Why will you win?
- 2x2 matrix showing you in the top-right corner.
- Honest about competitors. "No competitors" = no market OR you are naive.
- Your unfair advantage: technology, distribution, team, network effects, data moat.

### Slide 10: Team
Why are you the right people?
- 3-4 key people. Photo, name, role, one-line credential.
- The credential must be RELEVANT. "Ex-Google" is not a credential. "Ex-Google AI, led the team that reduced TPU training costs by 40%" is.
- If you have advisors, name the top 1-2.

### Slide 11: The Ask
What do you need?
- Amount you are raising.
- What you will achieve with it (12-18 month runway to next milestone).
- Current commitments ("already have $X committed from Y").

### Slide 12: The Vision
If you succeed, what does the world look like?
- One sentence. Aspirational but grounded.
- Not "we will be a $100B company." Think: "Every ML team trains models at half the cost, making AI accessible to anyone with an idea, not just anyone with a compute budget."

---

## Pitch Deck Design Rules

1. **One message per slide.** If a slide has two messages, split it.
2. **Max 30 words per slide.** If you need more, put them in the appendix.
3. **Font size 28pt minimum.** If it does not fit at 28pt, you have too much text.
4. **Dark background + white text** for projection. **White background + dark text** for screen sharing.
5. **Consistent visual language.** One color palette, one font family, one slide layout.
6. **No bullet-point walls.** Each bullet is a complete thought, not a sentence fragment.
7. **Investors read left-to-right, top-to-bottom.** Put the most important thing in the top-left.

---

## The Appendix (Not in the Main Deck)

Have these slides ready for Q&A:
- Detailed financial projections
- Customer case studies
- Technical architecture overview
- Extended team bios
- Competitive feature comparison matrix
- Go-to-market plan
- Risk and mitigation table

---

## Pitch Delivery Rules

1. **Never read your slides.** The slides support you, not the other way around.
2. **The first 30 seconds determine engagement.** Open with the problem, not your bio.
3. **Practice the 2-minute and 5-minute version.** Most pitches get cut short.
4. **Answer objections before they are asked.** If your biggest weakness is "no revenue yet," address it proactively.
5. **End with the ask.** Do not trail off. "We are raising $2M. I'd love to continue the conversation."

---

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] One-liner understandable in 5 seconds
- [ ] Problem quantified with a specific number
- [ ] Insight clear (why you, why now)
- [ ] Traction shown with a graph (even if small)
- [ ] Competition slide honest and differentiated
- [ ] The ask is specific (amount, use of funds, current commitments)
- [ ] Appendix ready for Q&A
- [ ] Deck works as a standalone PDF (someone should understand it without you presenting)
