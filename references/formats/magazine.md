# Magazine Article Format  --  Writing for General Audiences

## Goal

Translate technical work into prose that a smart non-expert reads to the end. Magazine articles are not simplified papers. They are a different genre entirely. The reader is intelligent but unfamiliar with your field. They will not tolerate jargon, equations, or "furthermore." They want to be fascinated.

## When to Use

- Nature News & Views, Science Perspectives
- The Atlantic, Wired, MIT Technology Review, Quanta Magazine
- Harvard Business Review (for business/management angles)
- Aeon, Nautilus (for philosophical/scientific essays)
- Trade publications (IEEE Spectrum, Communications of the ACM)
- Your company blog when targeting non-technical decision-makers

## Magazine vs Paper  --  The Difference

| Aspect | Research Paper | Magazine Article |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Task definition + challenge | Anecdote, statistic, or provocation |
| Language | Precise, technical, passive voice OK | Conversational, vivid, active voice |
| Equations | Welcome | Forbidden |
| Citations | Required, extensive | None or 1-2 endnotes |
| Length | 8-14 pages | 800-2500 words |
| Reader's goal | Learn a specific result | Understand why something matters |
| Structure | IMRaD (Intro, Method, Results, Discussion) | Narrative arc (hook → context → depth → human element → takeaway) |
| Figures | Technical charts, tables | Photographs, illustrations, infographics |
| Author | Researcher | Journalist or researcher writing for general audience |

## The Magazine Article Structure

### 1. The Hook (Paragraph 1  --  100 words)
Make the reader NEED to know more.
```
Options:
- A surprising statistic: "92% of developers use AI tools. But a structural
  flaw means none of them can trust the code."
- A provocative question: "What if the biggest bottleneck in AI isn't compute
  power — but something as simple as the cables connecting GPUs?"
- A human anecdote: "When Sarah's team trained their model last month,
  they discovered something strange. The GPUs spent more time waiting for
  each other than actually computing."
- A vivid image: "Inside a data center, thousands of GPUs blink in unison.
  Every second, they exchange billions of numbers. Most of those numbers
  are zeros."
```

### 2. The Context (2-3 paragraphs  --  300 words)
Why does this matter NOW?
- What changed in the world that makes this important?
- Who is affected?
- What's at stake?

### 3. The Deep Dive (4-6 paragraphs  --  800 words)
The technical content, explained through:
- **Metaphors**: "Attention is like a spotlight  --  the model learns where to point it."
- **Analogies**: "Training a large model is like conducting an orchestra where every musician needs to hear every other musician, and the sound takes longer to travel than the music."
- **Step-by-step**: "First, we realized X. Then, we tried Y. That failed because Z. Finally, we discovered W."
- **No equations**: Replace them with "roughly," "about," "imagine that..."

### 4. The Human Element (2-3 paragraphs  --  300 words)
Who benefits? Who is affected?
- A quote from a researcher or user.
- A concrete example of what this enables.
- A story about the discovery process.

### 5. The Takeaway (1 paragraph  --  100 words)
What should the reader do, think, or question after reading?
- One memorable sentence that captures the entire article.
- A forward-looking statement: "If this works at scale..."

## Magazine Writing Rules

### Do
- Open with the most interesting thing. Do not build up to it.
- Short paragraphs. 2-4 sentences. White space is inviting.
- Use "you" and "we." Include the reader.
- Explain ONE concept deeply rather than five concepts superficially.
- End paragraphs with a hook to the next one.
- Read your draft aloud. If you stumble, rewrite.

### Don't
- Use any of: "furthermore," "moreover," "hence," "thus," "consequently."
- Include equations, algorithms, or pseudocode.
- Assume the reader knows your field's vocabulary.
- Cite papers in the text. Put 1-2 "Further Reading" links at the end.
- Use passive voice: "It was observed that..." → "We saw that..."
- Hedge: "Our results suggest..." → "We found..." (Magazines are not peer review.)

## Pitching a Magazine Editor

Magazine articles are PITCHED before they are WRITTEN.

### The Pitch Email
```
Subject: Pitch: [One sentence that is the article's hook]

Dear [Editor Name],

[The HOOK — 1 sentence that creates curiosity]

[The STORY — 2 sentences explaining what happened and why it matters]

[The CREDENTIAL — 1 sentence on why you're the person to write this]

I'd love to write this as a [800/1500/2000]-word piece for [Section Name].

Best,
[Your Name]
```

### Magazine Lead Times
| Magazine | Lead Time | Pitch To |
|---|---|---|
| Nature News & Views | 4-8 weeks | Commissioned (they invite you) |
| Quanta Magazine | 4-8 weeks | Section editor |
| MIT Tech Review | 6-12 weeks | Section editor |
| Wired | 8-12 weeks | Section editor |
| The Atlantic | 8-16 weeks | Section editor |
| HBR | 8-16 weeks | Section editor |
| IEEE Spectrum | 4-8 weeks | Section editor |
| Trade publications | 2-4 weeks | Editor-in-chief |

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Hook creates compulsion to continue in the first 100 words
- [ ] Zero equations, algorithms, or pseudocode
- [ ] Zero "furthermore," "moreover," "hence," "thus," "consequently"
- [ ] At least one metaphor or analogy that makes the unfamiliar familiar
- [ ] At least one human story, quote, or concrete example
- [ ] Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences)
- [ ] Takeaway is one memorable sentence
- [ ] Read aloud and flows naturally
- [ ] Pitch email sent to editor BEFORE writing the full article
