# Does My Writing Flow? — Readability and Fluidity Diagnostics

## Goal

Good writing is not about complexity. It is about clarity, rhythm, and forward momentum. The reader's attention is a depleting resource. Every sentence that is unpleasant to read steals attention from the next sentence. This guide enforces the readability rules that make writing a pleasure to read, not a tax to endure.

---

## Quick Readability Test

For any paragraph, ask these 5 questions before moving on:

1. Can I read it aloud without stumbling or running out of breath?
2. Do sentence lengths vary, or are they all the same length?
3. Is at least 80% of it in active voice?
4. Does each sentence connect to the previous one with a clear logical relationship?
5. Can I remove any word without changing the meaning?

If any answer is "no," revise before continuing.

## The Seven Rules of Fluid Writing

### Rule 1: Vary Sentence Length

Monotony kills reading. Three long sentences in a row exhaust the reader. Three short sentences in a row feel choppy. The fix is deliberate variation.

```
BAD (all long):
The developing brain builds itself through a process of synaptic overproduction
followed by experience-dependent pruning, during which connections that are
frequently activated survive while those that are rarely recruited are eliminated,
and this process, which occurs most rapidly between ages 2 and 12, determines
which neural circuits the child retains into adulthood.

FIXED (short-medium-long rhythm):
The developing brain overproduces synapses and then eliminates the ones it does
not use. Connections that fire frequently survive. Connections that rarely fire
are pruned. This process, which occurs most rapidly between ages 2 and 12,
determines which neural circuits the child retains into adulthood.
```

**Rule of thumb**: No more than two consecutive sentences of similar length. After two long sentences, a short one. After two short sentences, a longer one that synthesizes.

- Short (4-8 words): Impact, punch, statement of fact.
- Medium (12-22 words): Development, explanation, evidence.
- Long (25-40 words): Synthesis, connection, qualification.

### Rule 2: Active Voice Dominance

Active voice creates forward momentum. The subject acts. The sentence moves. Passive voice reverses the flow: the recipient of the action becomes the subject, and the actor disappears or hides behind a "by" phrase.

```
PASSIVE (weak):
The data was analyzed by our team. (7 words)
ACTIVE (strong):
Our team analyzed the data. (5 words)

PASSIVE (vague):
It is believed that screen time impairs attention. (8 words)
ACTIVE (precise):
Three meta-analyses conclude that screen time impairs attention. (9 words)

PASSIVE (hiding agency):
Mistakes were made. (3 words)
ACTIVE (owning agency):
We made mistakes. (3 words)
```

**Rule of thumb**: At least 80% of sentences in active voice. Passive voice is acceptable when:
- The actor is unknown or irrelevant: "The cave was painted 30,000 years ago."
- The recipient is more important than the actor: "The patient was administered the treatment."
- You want to soften a claim: "The effect was observed in 60% of participants."

Never use passive voice to hide agency. Say who did what.

### Rule 3: Eliminate Filler

Filler phrases add syllables and remove nothing. They are the verbal equivalent of empty calories. Cut them.

| Filler Phrase | Replacement |
|---|---|
| "It is worth noting that" | Delete. Just state the fact. |
| "Interestingly," | Delete. If it is interesting, the reader will notice. |
| "In order to" | "To" |
| "It should be noted that" | Delete. Just state the fact. |
| "It is important to mention" | Delete. If it is important, the reader will know. |
| "The fact that" | "That" |
| "Due to the fact that" | "Because" |
| "In the event that" | "If" |
| "A total of" | Delete. "A total of 120 children" -> "120 children" |
| "Has the ability to" | "Can" |
| "Is capable of" | "Can" |
| "Conduct an analysis of" | "Analyze" |
| "Make a decision" | "Decide" |
| "Provide a description of" | "Describe" |

**Rule of thumb**: After writing a section, search for these phrases and delete them. Then re-read. The writing will be tighter, faster, and more pleasant.

### Rule 4: Concrete Over Abstract

Abstract language creates fog. Concrete language creates images. The reader's brain processes concrete words faster and remembers them longer.

```
ABSTRACT:
The early developmental period is characterized by significant neuroplasticity
that renders the organism particularly susceptible to environmental inputs.

CONCRETE:
Between birth and age 5, a child's brain forms and prunes billions of synapses.
Every song heard, every word spoken, every block stacked shapes which connections
survive.
```

**Rule of thumb**: After writing a paragraph, identify every abstract noun (development, plasticity, intervention, outcome, mechanism, phenomenon). Replace at least half of them with concrete equivalents (the child, the synapse, the drawing, the test score, the dopamine burst, the tower of blocks).

### Rule 5: Paragraph Rhythm

A paragraph has a shape. Opening sentence: short, clear, states the message. Middle sentences: develop, support, explain, qualify. Closing sentence: impact, synthesis, or transition.

```
BAD (no rhythm, all development, no shape):
The dopamine system is involved in learning. Dopamine neurons fire when a reward
is received. They also fire during anticipation. The anticipation phase is
important for motivation. Children need to experience anticipation to develop
motivation.

GOOD (topic sentence, development, closing punch):
Dopamine is not the pleasure molecule. It is the anticipation molecule. Dopamine
neurons begin firing before the reward arrives, during the pursuit, during the
effort. The longer the pursuit, the more dopamine is released, and the stronger
the reinforcement. Remove the effort, and you remove the signal that makes reward
meaningful.
```

**Rule of thumb**: A paragraph of 8+ sentences must earn every sentence. If the paragraph is that long, consider breaking it into two paragraphs with clear division of the idea.

### Rule 6: Transition Craft

Every sentence must connect to the previous one. The relationship must be clear. If it is not, add an explicit transition word.

| Relationship | Transition Words |
|---|---|
| Cause | "This happens because," "The reason is," "This arises from" |
| Contrast | "However," "In contrast," "But," "Yet," "On the other hand" |
| Consequence | "As a result," "Therefore," "Consequently," "This means that" |
| Refinement | "More specifically," "In particular," "That is," "To put it precisely" |
| Example | "For instance," "Consider," "Imagine," "Take the case of" |
| Addition | "Furthermore," "Moreover," "In addition" — use sparingly; prefer the other relationships |

**Anti-pattern**: A string of "Furthermore" or "Additionally" transitions. These indicate that the writer is stacking facts rather than building an argument. Each sentence should relate to the previous one through cause, contrast, consequence, refinement, or example, not mere juxtaposition.

### Rule 7: The Read-Aloud Test

The final and most important test. Read every paragraph aloud. Your ear catches what your eye misses.

- **Stumble**: If you stumble over a word or phrase, rewrite it. The reader will stumble too.
- **Breath**: If you run out of breath before the end of a sentence, the sentence is too long. Break it.
- **Robot voice**: If it sounds like a robot wrote it, it probably did. Add humanity. Add rhythm. Add a short punchy sentence.
- **Monotone**: If every sentence has the same cadence, vary the rhythm. Start a sentence with a short clause. End a sentence with impact. Break the pattern deliberately.

## Full Readability Diagnostic

Run this diagnostic on every section before finalizing. It takes 10 minutes and catches 90% of readability problems.

### Pass 1: Sentence Length Scan
- Count words per sentence for one full page.
- If three consecutive sentences are within 3 words of each other in length, rewrite one.
- If any sentence exceeds 40 words, break it or restructure it.

### Pass 2: Active Voice Audit
- Count passive voice constructions (any form of "to be" + past participle: "was analyzed," "were observed," "is believed").
- If passive voice exceeds 20% of sentences, rewrite the passives.
- Flag any passive that hides agency and rewrite.

### Pass 3: Filler Hunt
- Search for: "It is worth noting," "Interestingly," "In order to," "It should be noted," "It is important to," "The fact that," "Due to the fact that," "A total of," "Has the ability to," "Is capable of," "Conduct an analysis of," "Make a decision about," "Provide a description of."
- Delete or replace every instance.

### Pass 4: Abstract-to-Concrete Ratio
- Count abstract nouns on one page (words ending in -tion, -ity, -ness, -ment, -ence, -ance, -ism, -ology).
- If abstract nouns exceed 15% of total words, replace half with concrete equivalents.

### Pass 5: Read Aloud
- Read the entire section aloud. Mark every sentence where you stumble or run out of breath.
- Rewrite every marked sentence.
- Read aloud again. Repeat until smooth.

### Pass 6: Topic Sentence Extract
- Copy the first sentence of every paragraph into a separate document.
- Read only those sentences. Do they tell a coherent, logical story?
- If not, reorder paragraphs or rewrite topic sentences.

## Red Flags Checklist

Before finalizing any document, verify none of these are present:

- [ ] Three or more consecutive sentences of similar length
- [ ] Any sentence over 40 words
- [ ] Passive voice exceeding 20% of sentences
- [ ] Filler phrases (see Pass 3 above)
- [ ] Abstract noun density exceeding 15%
- [ ] Paragraphs without a clear topic sentence
- [ ] Paragraphs exceeding 8 sentences without justification
- [ ] "Furthermore" or "Additionally" used as the primary transition
- [ ] Topic sentences buried at the END of paragraphs
- [ ] Two different ideas crammed into one paragraph
- [ ] Any paragraph that fails the read-aloud test

If any box is checked, the document is not finished.

## Example: Before and After

**BEFORE (89 words, 3 sentences, all passive/abstract)**:
> The impact of generative artificial intelligence on the developmental trajectory
> of children is a subject of increasing concern among researchers in the field of
> developmental psychology. It has been observed by clinicians that fine motor delays
> are becoming more prevalent in pediatric populations, which may be attributable to
> the displacement of traditional drawing activities by AI-generated content. The
> importance of addressing this phenomenon cannot be overstated.

**AFTER (72 words, 5 sentences, active/concrete, varied rhythm)**:
> Generative AI is changing how children develop. Clinicians report a pattern that
> did not exist five years ago: five-year-olds who can describe what they want to
> draw but cannot hold a pencil. They have not practiced drawing. They have practiced
> prompting. The product exists. The developmental work did not happen.

The AFTER version is shorter, clearer, more vivid, and more persuasive. It uses varied sentence lengths (6, 12, 6, 5, 8 words). Every sentence is in active voice. Every word earns its place. That is the standard.

## Integration With the Skill

- Run this diagnostic as Phase C, Step 12, after claim-evidence check and before clean dashes.
- Apply to ALL formats. Readability is format-agnostic.
- For long documents, run the diagnostic section by section. Don't wait until the end.
- The read-aloud test is non-negotiable. If you do nothing else, do this.
